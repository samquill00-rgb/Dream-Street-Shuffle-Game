Autumn, 1973. Soho, London. You've written a book and you've a baby at home; tonight, somehow, you'll make them both make sense. The book took much longer to make than the girl, though after an immediate, impromptu effort, you can't take much credit for her. The girl is born now and her mother, Aoife, sits at home, with the babe in her arms. Home's cold; at home there's not much food. If she's told you once, she's told you again and again that there's not enough money. You are not a bad man so you've taken a job in a warehouse close to home. But it won't bring in enough, not all year. And so tonight, you've gone back to Soho, with a book in your bag and a sense of the place as it was, ten years ago, when no wife, no house or land still seemed quite natural. You'll try, you suppose, the haunts you used to haunt and the people you knew. That'll work: it's a good book and God's a good man. You've been thinking about the form of the aubade, the morning poem, the song of wake-me-not-so-I-might-have-dream. You've been thinking about the first love of your life; Lily, from the deepest vale of Wales. Forget about your book, an aubade is a song to the dawn, and the only way out of Soho is to find one.
