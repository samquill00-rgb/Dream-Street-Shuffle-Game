As you walk in the bar drops down to silence, as if its speech were interrupted by a quick gasp for breath, the way an antelope sometimes mistakes the wind in the grass for a lion, stiffens, then goes about its day.

At one end of the bar are the artists; you know them, they are famous.

Stuffed into a barstool which his arsecheeks overhang, like clouds waiting to rain, is a stout man with his back to you. His leather jacket, somehow both parched and sweating, falls short at the neck, flashing a collar of labial pink.

Opposite him is the only man who clocks you, who is thin like verdaccio and hardly there at all.
